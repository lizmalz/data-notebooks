# Statistical Sampling: The Core Idea

Sampling is the process of selecting a subset of a population to make
inferences about the whole. Instead of measuring every unit (which is
often impossible or inefficient), you measure a representative portion
and use probability theory to generalize your findings.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

## Why Sampling Works

Sampling works because of three pillars:

**1. Randomness**

Every unit has a known, non‑zero chance of being selected. This prevents
bias.

**2. Representativeness**

A well‑designed sample mirrors the structure of the population.

**3. Probability Theory**

Sampling distributions, confidence intervals, and error bounds allow you
to quantify uncertainty.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

## Major Types of Sampling

**1. Probability Sampling (Statistically rigorous)**

These methods allow you to compute sampling error and confidence
intervals.

**Simple Random Sampling**

- Every unit has equal chance of selection

- Best when population is homogeneous

- Example: randomly selecting 500 polling units from a national list

**Systematic Sampling**

- Select every *k*th unit after a random start

- Efficient for ordered lists

- Risk: periodic patterns can bias results

**Stratified Sampling**

- Divide population into strata (e.g., regions, income groups)

- Sample within each stratum

- Reduces variance and ensures representation

- Ideal for heterogeneous populations

**Cluster Sampling**

- Select clusters (e.g., villages, census blocks) instead of individuals

- Useful when population is geographically dispersed

- Higher sampling error unless clusters are internally diverse

**Multistage Sampling**

- Combine methods (e.g., select districts → select wards → select
  households)

- Standard in national surveys and election audits

------------------------------------------------------------------------

## 2. Non‑Probability Sampling (Not statistically generalizable)

Useful for exploratory work, but cannot compute sampling error.

**Convenience Sampling**

Easy to access units (e.g., online surveys)

**Purposive/Judgment Sampling**

Expert selects units based on criteria

**Quota Sampling**

Fill quotas for categories without random selection

**Snowball Sampling**

Used for hard‑to‑reach populations (e.g., informal networks)

------------------------------------------------------------------------

## Key Concepts You Should Know

### Sample Size

Determined by:

- Desired confidence level (e.g., 95%)

- Margin of error (e.g., ±3%)

- Population variability

- Population size (minor effect unless population is small)

### Sampling Error

Difference between sample estimate and true population value\
Quantified using standard error formulas

### Bias

Systematic deviation caused by poor sampling design\
Examples: undercoverage, nonresponse, selection bias

### Weighting

Adjusts sample to match population structure\
Essential in stratified and complex surveys
